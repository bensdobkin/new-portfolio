Past-Due and Recovery for the Medicare Prescription Payment Plan

2024–2025

Summary

I designed the end-to-end past-due and recovery experience for a no-interest federal program, covering both the member-facing portal and the advocate-facing internal tool. The work was in service of contractually committed delinquency targets of 5% for Humana and 15% for Express Scripts, and had to achieve those targets without the usual financial levers: no late fees, no interest, no credit reporting. The shipped system drove a roughly 7% improvement in payment method adoption across active members and a measurable lift in advocate completion of the post-opt-in setup flow.

Problem

The Medicare Prescription Payment Plan is a no-interest program. Members who fall behind cannot be charged late fees, cannot accrue interest, and cannot be reported to credit bureaus. The program is designed to help, not to punish. But the commercial reality is that Paytient signed contractual delinquency targets with its sponsors: 5% for Humana and 15% for Express Scripts, measured as the dollar volume of open past-due balances.

The scale of the problem was visible in the data early. Of roughly 41,000 active members — members who had picked up at least one prescription under the program — only about 11,000 had a payment method on file, and only around 10,000 were enrolled in autopay. Initial projections had 60% of payment volume coming through checks, which are asynchronous, delay-prone, and correlate with delinquency. Humana's advocate behavior told a more specific story: after opting a member into the program, 95% of Humana advocates went straight back to the generic search page without clicking through to payments. Express Scripts advocates completed the payment setup 36% of the time.

The problem was not that members did not want to pay. The problem was that the program had no built-in mechanism to enforce payment, opt-in alone was being treated as the end of the workflow by the people helping members enroll, and the product needed to close both gaps at once without resorting to coercive design.

Insight

The insight came out of reframing what recovery actually is in this product. A traditional billing product uses late fees and interest to motivate repayment. MPPP could not. That left three levers: what the member sees, what the advocate can do, and what the product itself can translate between them.

Recovery in MPPP had to happen across two audiences working together. The member needed to see the consequences of their situation clearly enough to act without feeling punished. The advocate needed the right information at the right moment to help, without having to remote-control the member's screen to do it. And the product itself had to do a surprising amount of the work — turning machine-level signals like Stripe decline codes into human-level guidance the advocate could actually use on the phone.

This shifted the design problem. The goal was not to push members harder. The goal was to make the path to repayment visible, accurate, and supported on both sides of the call.

Process

Understanding the two-audience shape

The data separated the problem into two distinct design tracks. On the member side, the question was how to communicate a past-due state that felt honest rather than punitive, and how to give members a clear path to action at every stage — missed payment, failed payment, partial payment, opt-out with outstanding balance. On the advocate side, the question was what information advocates needed to actually help, and how to deliver it without making them toggle into the member's portal to see anything.

The second question was more interesting because it pointed to a larger product decision. The original model had been for advocates to impersonate the member's portal when a member called in for help. That had been technically simpler but operationally weak: advocates had incomplete tools, the workflow was awkward on calls, and the compliance posture was not ideal. Feedback from advocates repeatedly asked for a standalone experience with everything the member sees plus more. That advocate-side work became its own full product, the advocate portal, which is covered in a separate case study. For this case study, the relevant piece is that past-due handling on the advocate side is native to that tool rather than an overlay on the member's screen.

Designing for decline codes

The single most consequential piece of detail work was in failed payments. When a payment fails, the payment processor returns a machine-readable decline code. Raw codes like authentication_required, card_velocity_exceeded, expired_card, and processing_error are technically accurate but useless to an advocate on a call with a 72-year-old member.

I mapped each of approximately 30 Stripe decline codes to three things: a high-level next step the advocate should take (prompt for another payment method, retry after a minute, prompt for accurate details, or escalate to the card issuer), plain-language support guidance, and where helpful, a scripted sentence the advocate could read directly to the member. The raw code is never surfaced — the advocate sees the action and the script, nothing more.

Raw code from processor What the advocate sees
expired_card Prompt for accurate details. "It looks like the card we have on file expired for you. Would you like me to update your card with the new expiration details or add a new payment method to use?"
insufficient_funds Prompt for another payment method. "It looks like the card we have on file for you is no longer valid. Would you like me to add another payment method?"
processing_error Retry payment after a minute. "It looks like there was an error processing the payment. If we could try the transaction in a minute, we can re-attempt this transaction."
duplicate_transaction Retry after a minute — but first navigate to the payment log and verify no matching transaction was made within the last 60 seconds, to avoid overpayment.
fraudulent Prompt for another payment method. Intentionally vague, to avoid giving actionable information to a bad actor impersonating an advocate.

The fraudulent-code treatment was a real design constraint. If decline messaging had been too descriptive, a bad actor could have used that information to exploit the member's financial setup. The system had to be detailed enough for legitimate advocates to help, and vague enough to be safe if abused. Every message was reviewed with that balance in mind.

The refund-as-transfer decision

Refunds became one of the most interesting design problems in the recovery system. The obvious default would have been to let advocates process refunds directly as checks. But a refund has two meanings for an MPPP member. Sometimes the member actually wants their money back. Often, the member has misapplied a payment — for example, sending a Medicare Premium payment to the MPPP lockbox address — and what they actually want is for the money to end up in the right place.

I designed the refund flow to ask "where should this money go" before processing anything. The advocate sees three options: Member Refund, Medicare Premium, or Centerwell Pharmacy (the sponsor's in-house pharmacy). Each option explains when to use it. When the advocate selects a transfer type, the portal surfaces a script they read to the member explaining that the outstanding balance will adjust, along with a clear disclosure that a cash refund, if applicable, would arrive by check within roughly 14 days.

Decision

The refund-as-transfer pattern was the decision that most directly expressed the insight. A simpler design would have processed refunds as cash and left the member to re-send the funds to the right place, losing both time and money in the round trip. A more aggressive design would have refused refunds outright or routed them silently. The shipped pattern respects the member's autonomy by making every option visible, keeps funds inside the sponsor's ecosystem when the member's real need is reconciliation, and gives the advocate the language to explain what each choice means. It converts a revenue-leak moment into a reconciliation moment, without coercing anyone.

Solution

The shipped system works across the member and advocate sides of the product, with specific surfaces for each stage of the recovery journey.

The member-facing past-due state

The home page is the primary communication surface. When the member is past due, the entire top section re-colors to red, the payment due date is replaced with a "Payment was due" label, and a banner running across the top of the page explains the stakes in plain language — including the risk of removal from the program. The underlying layout is unchanged, which keeps the experience navigable for members who might be alarmed by the shift.

Failed and partial payments

When a payment fails, the member sees a short, clear modal rather than any technical error. When a member pays less than the minimum due, the confirmation screen shows what was paid and what remains — with the remaining past due explicitly called out in red and the date by which the rest must be paid to stay in the program. The design principle throughout is to be honest about consequences without being punitive about presentation.

Opt-out with outstanding balance

The opt-out flow was a distinct design problem. A member who wants to leave the program but has not yet paid their balance cannot simply be stopped from opting out. They also cannot be allowed to walk away without acknowledging the debt they still owe. The shipped design uses a calendar grid of the member's actual year, with paid months in green, to make the outstanding balance feel concrete rather than abstract. The pay-in-full option is clearly offered. The alternative is a red button that commits the member to continued monthly payments as an explicit choice, not a default.

The advocate-facing recovery tools

Advocates see past-due status on entering a member's record, with the amount owed, the grace period countdown, and a direct prompt to ask the member to make a payment. Detailed payment status — including the translated decline codes and suggested next steps — is available without needing to impersonate the member's portal.

The refund-as-transfer flow closes the loop by routing overpayments to the right place in the sponsor's system whenever the member's actual need is reconciliation rather than cash in hand.

Result

The shipped system was designed to meet the contractually committed delinquency targets of 5% for Humana and 15% for Express Scripts. After rollout, payment method adoption across active members improved by roughly 7%, and the advocate side saw a meaningful lift in post-opt-in completion — closing the Humana gap where 95% of advocates had previously dropped off without setting up payment methods. The refund-as-transfer flow reduced cash refund volume, keeping funds inside the sponsor's ecosystem in cases where the member's real need was reconciliation.

What mattered was not that the product collected more aggressively — a no-interest program cannot. What mattered was that the product made the path to repayment visible on both sides of the call, translated machine signals into human action, and treated refund requests as reconciliation questions rather than cash-outs. In a product designed to help seniors afford their medication, recovery had to feel like help, not punishment, and the shipped system is an argument that it could.

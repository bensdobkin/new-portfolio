Summary

I designed a standalone internal product for customer service advocates who support Medicare Prescription Payment Plan members across Humana, Express Scripts, and Careplus. The portal replaced an earlier workflow in which advocates impersonated the member's own portal to provide support — a model that was slow, friction-heavy, and poorly suited to a senior population. The shipped tool reduced average call time by approximately 38% and gave advocates direct control over the workflows they use most: enrollment, payment methods, autopay, payments, claims lookup, and refund handling.

Problem

When Paytient launched the Medicare Prescription Payment Plan, customer service advocates at each sponsor were the humans on the other end of every confused call. A 72-year-old member calling in because she did not understand her bill, because her autopay failed, or because she wanted to opt out, was routed to an advocate trained on the program. The advocate's job was to resolve the issue on the call, and the company measured advocates on the time it took them to do so.

The original support model had advocates impersonating the member's portal. When a member called in, the advocate would authenticate into a view of that member's own account, navigate the member-facing interface, and guide the call from there. This was fast to build because it reused an experience that already existed. In practice, it failed in several ways at once. The member's portal was designed for a senior managing their own account once a month, not for an operator managing a hundred accounts a day. Advocates could see only what members could see — missing the confirmation numbers, participation logs, opt-in channels, reason codes, and eligibility dates they needed to actually resolve a call. And the authentication flow itself was a friction point: members often did not remember their email or password, and even a two-minute credential recovery was a meaningful percentage of an advocate's handle time budget.

The support model needed to be rebuilt as its own product.

Before — impersonation
Advocates authenticated into the member's portal using the member's credentials, and credential recovery alone could eat minutes of call time.
Information density was tuned for a senior managing one account a month, not an operator managing a hundred a day.
Advocates could see only what members could see — no confirmation numbers, no reason codes, no opt-in channels.
No direct write access to refunds, autopay toggles, or payment method edits that the advocate's workflow required.

Every member-side action available natively — enrollment, payment methods, autopay, payments, refunds.

Insight

The insight was recognizing that advocates were users of their own product, not operators of someone else's. A member-facing product is designed for a senior managing a single relationship with a program — accessed occasionally, with deliberately thinned information density so the experience feels simple. An advocate-facing product is the opposite: the primary workspace for someone managing dozens of relationships a day, accessed constantly, where density, lookup speed, write access, and decision support are the entire point.

Impersonation had papered over this by reusing a member's interface for an advocate's workflow. The design brief was to build the tool that advocates actually needed, which would look, feel, and behave differently from the product members used — because the two audiences have nothing in common except the data underneath.

The decision point was whether to invest in a parallel product or continue extending impersonation. Extending impersonation would have been cheaper in the short term. Building a parallel product meant designing a full information architecture, a full action model, and a full set of workflows from scratch. The evidence that pushed the decision was the friction data: advocates reported the impersonation flow as their single biggest time sink, credential recovery alone was costing minutes per call, and the call-time metric advocates were measured on was directly affected.

Process

Listening to the actual users

I spent time with customer service advocates and their leadership at both Humana and Express Scripts to understand the shape of a support call, the moments where impersonation failed, and the actions advocates needed to take that the member's portal would not let them.

A few patterns emerged. Advocates needed to look up members by Medicare Beneficiary Identifier or member ID, not by email. They needed to see participation history — when someone opted in, by what channel, and what confirmation number was issued. They needed to take action on the member's behalf: enrolling a new member, unenrolling a member, adding or editing a payment method, enabling or disabling autopay, making a payment, and processing refunds. They needed session tracking for compliance and quality assurance. And they needed all of this without leaving the page or authenticating as the member.

None of this was possible in impersonation.

Information architecture

I structured the portal around the domains of information advocates actually worked in, rather than around member-facing tasks. The member portal is organized around things a member does — home, claims, payments, account. The advocate portal is organized around data domains an operator needs to pull from quickly.

Section What it does
Eligibility & Participation Part D eligibility, participation status, opt-in and opt-out history with channels, confirmation numbers, and reason codes.
Claims Prescription claim history, outstanding balance, year-to-date spend, and adjusted or reversed claim visibility.
Payments Payment schedule, payment log, saved payment methods, autopay management, refund handling, and quick actions for payment methods, autopay, and payments.
Member Details Personal details, account information, payment methods including closed and expired accounts, and Medicare identifiers.

A member can see a subset of this. An advocate can see all of it, edit most of it, and act on the member's behalf within it. The deliberate asymmetry between the two portals is the whole point.

Scope expansion from feedback

The first version of the product was narrow. It covered member details and enrollment and not much else. Advocates could see a member's basic information and opt them into the program, but anything else required falling back to impersonation. The team originally intended to keep impersonation as the primary workflow and the advocate portal as a supporting tool.

Direct feedback from advocates changed the plan. Multiple rounds of input from customer service leadership at Humana and Express Scripts pointed in the same direction: advocates wanted to stop impersonating members entirely, and they wanted every action a member could take to be available directly in the advocate tool. The scope expanded over several releases to cover payment method management, autopay enrollment, payment processing, refund handling, and detailed payment status including translated decline codes from the payment processor.

The commitment that defined the final product: an advocate should never have to enter the member's portal. Whatever the member can do, the advocate can do from here, with the additional information and write access their job requires.

Decision

The decision that shaped the whole product was to invest in a standalone parallel experience rather than continue extending impersonation. Extending impersonation would have been incrementally faster in the short term but would have preserved the authentication friction, the density mismatch, and the write-access limitations that were costing advocates time on every call. Building a parallel product required designing a full information architecture and action model from scratch, but it directly addressed the metric advocates were measured on. The measurable outcome — a 38% reduction in average call time — validated the decision.

Solution

The shipped advocate portal is a purpose-built internal product covering every action an advocate takes during a support call — with the information density and direct-action capability that member-facing products are designed to avoid.

Direct member lookup and context

Advocates enter a member view by searching for the member's identifier, with no authentication of the member required. Every session is tracked with a copyable session ID for compliance and call-quality review.

Eligibility and participation workflows

This section gives advocates the context that members themselves do not typically see or understand: which specific Part D plan the member is on, when they opted in, through what channel, with what confirmation number, and every participation status change over time. The opt-out control sits in this section so advocates can process opt-outs directly during the call that initiated the request.

Claims visibility

Advocates can see every claim, its status, its amount, and the pharmacy it originated from. The outstanding balance and year-to-date spend cards are surfaced prominently because they are the numbers advocates are asked about most often during calls.

Payments and payment method management

Payments is the densest section of the product and the one that took the most iteration to get right. Advocates can add or remove payment methods on behalf of the member, enroll or disable autopay, process one-time payments, view the full history of every payment attempt with confirmation numbers and reason codes, and initiate refunds. This is the section that most directly replaced impersonation — every payment-related action that previously required the advocate to drive the member's screen is now native here.

Advocate-only features

Beyond feature parity with the member portal, the advocate side has meaningful capabilities members do not have access to: translated payment processor decline codes with scripted guidance for the advocate to read to the member, a refund flow that offers reconciliation routing alternatives (Medicare Premium and Centerwell Pharmacy) rather than defaulting to cash refunds, and a past-due banner that surfaces at the top of the member view with a direct link to initiate a payment. These features are covered in more detail in the past-due and recovery case study.

Result

The shipped advocate portal reduced average call time by approximately 38%. Advocates are measured on call time, so this number translates directly to operational capacity for each sponsor. Humana, Express Scripts, and Careplus all use the product today.

The 38% number matters because it is the tightest possible validation of the insight. If advocates were genuinely just users of a tool that happened to serve a different workflow, giving them their own product should have moved their metric significantly. It did. The design decision that looked expensive in the short term — building a parallel product rather than extending impersonation — was the decision that changed the operational picture of the whole support operation. The broader lesson is that when operators spend their entire workday inside your product, they are your users too, and the version of the product they need looks nothing like the one the end customer uses.

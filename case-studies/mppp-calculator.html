Monthly Payment Calculator for the Medicare Prescription Payment Plan

2024

I created a calculator that lets Medicare Part D members estimate what they'll pay each month under the new federal Medicare Prescription Payment Plan.

Role

Founding Product Designer

Agency

Paytient

Client

Humana
Express Scripts
Cigna

Shipped

October 2024

My role

I was the Founding Product Designer on this project to ship this work. I started alongside a Product Manager and Staff Engineer to build this product and the design system from the ground up in less than 10 months.

Context

The Medicare Prescription Payment Plan was a net-new federal program that allowed seniors on Part D to spread their out-of-pocket prescription costs over the calendar year instead of paying in full at the pharmacy.

Paytient stepped in to create a white-labeled product offering to support the end-to-end needs from this member facing portal to a calculator tool and CRM portal for customer reps to use when supporting members.

Problem

Before a member enrolled in the new program, they needed to know whether it would actually help them manage their prescription costs.

On top of that, the math was not intuitive. The benefit of the program depended on your prescription costs over the calendar year, how early you hit the $2,000 out-of-pocket maximum, and when you enrolled in the program. Without a way to see that clearly, seniors who would benefit were likely to dismiss the program, and seniors who would not benefit were likely to sign up and be confused when their monthly bills did not match their expectations.

Insight

The Medicare population we interviewed was budget-conscious regardless of income, with participants consistently practicing monthly budgeting and often comparing the program to the "level pay" plans they used for utility bills. They were not looking for a tool that sold them on the program. Instead, they were looking for a tool that respected their actual spending pattern and told them honestly what their monthly costs would be if they enrolled.

Process

Competitive audit

I audited 23 calculators across six categories: employee cost, medical, mortgage, car loan, buy-now-pay-later, and loan. The most instructive references clustered around a handful of patterns that mapped to what our users needed and what they didn't.

Calculator What it did well Where it fell short
Klarna BNPL Radically simple interaction; sheet treatment kept users in flow. Almost no context around payment details.
Carvana Car Loan Calculation visible at top and bottom of screen; clean, minimal UI. Small type and tight padding would not work for older users.
Monarch Mortgage Card treatment made the calculator stand out from educational content; monthly payment updated dynamically as inputs changed. Mobile layout caused chart to take up a lot of vertical space.
Medicare Premium Calculator Educational definitions readily available; notes prominently displayed. Inconsistent CTA colors, small calculation values, weak hierarchy on results page.

Across the audit, the patterns that mattered for our users were large tap targets, high contrast, context underneath fields rather than behind info icons, and results that updated without requiring a second action.

Research with the target population

Paytient conducted 47 interviews with Medicare beneficiaries and caregivers across Missouri and other locations, with 11 participants aged 65 and up in moderated testing. Three findings shaped the calculator's direction.

First, participants consistently compared MPPP to the "level pay" utility plans they already used — not to loans or financing. Second, every participant responded positively to viewing a table of future payments, finding it helpful for budgeting. Third, most remote participants joined sessions on tablets, which meant the calculator output needed to work on a screen where the full year could be visible at once without scrolling.

Explorations

I explored four directions before landing on the shipped design.

Direction What it did Why it fell short
Single-page direct input All monthly input fields and the output on one scrolling page. Maximized specificity but front-loaded a dense form before users saw any value.
Two-question range Asked only for total prescription cost and year-to-date spend; returned a range like $150–$210. Simple, but the range felt uncertain and did not show how the year would actually unfold.
Table list Led with a hero number for the first monthly payment, with a collapsible list of every month below. Privileged the first payment over the full picture; required scrolling on smaller screens.
Calendar grid Split input and output into two steps: month-by-month entry on its own screen, output rendered as a three-column calendar with an OOP max progress bar. This is the direction that shipped.

Solution

The calendar grid won because it was the only direction that treated every month as equally important and showed the shape of the year at a glance. Other explorations misrepresented how the program actually works or were too vague for a population that wanted specificity.

In research, participants read the calendar grid as a calendar, which matched how they already thought about monthly budgeting. On tablets, the device most remote participants joined on, the full year fit in a single view without scrolling.

Result

The Medicare Prescription Payment Plan launched in October 2024 and is now available to roughly 20 million Medicare enrollees through Humana, Cigna, and other sponsor partners — about one third of the Medicare population. The calculator was the entry point for the program and the first surface prospective enrollees touched.

In user testing across 47 interviews, every participant successfully completed the calculator flow. Participants consistently described it as straightforward and easy to follow — a notable contrast with how the same participants described the broader Medicare enrollment experience as overwhelming and confusing.

What mattered was not just that the tool worked, but that it gave seniors an honest answer to a question they had been unable to ask before. For a population where one missed prescription could mean walking away from medication they needed, a clear view of what the year would cost was the thing that let them decide.
